## Your Year at NODE

*A month-by-month guide from "I'm in!" to rolling onto the playa.*

Getting to Burning Man is a season-long build. Here's the rhythm of a NODE year so you know what's coming and when. The camper calls ramp up as the burn gets closer. They're the best way to meet your campmates before you're sharing a shade structure with them.

### Winter (January-March)

At this point the NODE leadership team is reaching out to suppliers and starting to prepare past and future campers for Burning Man.

- A monthly call to get to know your fellow campers.
- **In March, Burning Man tickets go on sale.** Don't miss the on-sale dates.

### Spring (April-May)

Suppliers start to get booked and paid, and the camper calls pick up to every other week.

- Bi-weekly calls for campers.
- **Dues are due end of May.** (See *Membership & Dues*.)

### Early Summer (June-July)

You should have your flights and transportation arranged by now.

- Book your flights and transportation to and from the playa.
- Start picking out outfits and finalizing fun gift ideas.
- Weekly calls begin.
- Last chance for tickets. The FOMO is real.

### August (Almost There)

- Sign up for camp jobs and activities.
- Get to know your fellow campmates on the weekly calls.
- Do a final pass on your *Packing Checklist* before you roll out.

## Camp Orientation & Welcome Tour

*What your first walk through camp covers, plus a checklist for whoever's giving the tour.*

When you arrive on playa, someone will take you on a welcome tour of camp. It's how you learn where everything is, how camp runs, and the handful of norms that keep 50-ish people living happily in the dust together. Read it before you arrive so nothing's a surprise, and use it as your script if you're the one giving the tour.

### First things first

- Drop your bags at the entrance to the tent shade structure so your hands are free for the walk-through.
- Start with a positive note. Thank them for being part of NODE, and stress the importance of the whole *and* the individual. Instill a sense of ownership for the camp.

### Private (Dining) Tent

This is our home on the playa, so treat it as such.

- **Job schedule:** Find the big printed job board here. Your shifts and duties live on it, and throughout the tour we highlight the areas of camp where various jobs get done.
- **Trash management:** Note where the trash bins, dumpster, food-waste mesh bag, and liquid-disposal buckets are, and learn the sorting so trash duty is easy for everyone.
- **Keep it tidy:** Minimize personal items strewn around the private tent. We clear off the dining table for every meal, and anything left on it gets mercilessly removed.

### Kitchen

- **Dishwashing:** Learn the three-bin dish station, the process, and the purpose of each bin.
- **Meal schedule:** Know when meals happen (see *Membership & Dues* for what's included).
- **Snack tent:** There's a grab-n'-go / leftovers prep area. Help yourself.
- **Hydration:** Water coolers and electrolyte (Liquid IV) packets are here. *Camp norm: don't post Liquid Death on socials. Officially, it doesn't exist.* <!-- editor: confirm whether the Liquid Death note is still relevant -->

### Bathrooms & Showers

- Give them a walk-through of the bathroom trailer and the protocol for using the toilets.
- **Half-step on the flusher before you go #2.** 💩
- Reiterate cleanliness.

### Bike Parking

- If you bought an e-bike through NODE, it'll be waiting in the storage container. If it needs wheels or pedals attached, follow the assembly tutorials in the bike emails.
- **Bikes should never be brought through the public tent into the private area of camp.**

### Public Stretch Tent & Bar

This is NODE's portal to the rest of the event.

- When NODEs hang out here, camp looks welcoming, bustling, and sexy. Spend time here.
- No personal camper items should be left in the public tent, ever. Leave things at your own risk.

### Fire Pit & Swings

Tertiary camp amenities. If you're into a fire, ask where the wood stash is kept.

### Tent Shade Structure

- **Tent placement:** Check with camp leads on exactly where to pitch your tent within the shade structure.
- **Power:** Tell whoever's setting you up your expected energy usage and any extra electronics you brought, so we can manage the camp's power load.

### For whoever's giving the tour

Keep it warm and useful. Hit these beats:

- [ ] Greet new campers with warmth and a smile
- [ ] Direct all cars to camp parking spots
- [ ] Offer a wagon and a hand unloading
- [ ] Help them find their rental tent in the containers (if they confirmed a rental ahead of time)
- [ ] Show them where to set up in tent city
- [ ] Answer questions and ease nerves
- [ ] Stress that personal items shouldn't be left strewn around camp
- [ ] Help set the tone for a great burn
- [ ] Take a Polaroid for the camp photo board

## Membership & Dues

*How NODE thinks about money, what your dues cover, and what we ask in return.*

Welcome! This is really the most important page to pay attention to. It explains how membership works, our sliding-scale dues, and the commitments that make camp run.

### Becoming a member

Membership starts with the intake form. Fill it out to confirm your spot and give us what we need to plan.

- **Intake form:** [NODE Intake Form](https://form.typeform.com/to/ayziRuVw). **[NEEDS UPDATE: replace with current-year intake form link]**

### What's included

Included with the camp:

- **Food:** Breakfast and dinner daily throughout the event, brunch on strike day, and a pre-Temple-burn BBQ. Snacks and leftovers are always available, and a full breakfast/sandwich station (waffle maker, toasters, deep fryer, panini press, blenders) is open at any time. <!-- editor: exact meal start/end dates vary by year -->
- **Daily coffee**, cold brew on tap most mornings
- **Full bar**
- **Power grid**
- **Water** (filtered, at the camp fill station)
- **Private bathrooms**
- **Hot / cold showers**
- **Chill tent with A/C**
- **A camp gift**
- **A diverse, awesome group** of roughly 50 new and old friends who've come together to make something epic

### What we ask of you

Every NODE is committed to the following, no matter what they pay:

- **Shifts:** Approximately 4 x 3-hour shifts on playa (the shift schedule comes out mid-summer). A pattern of incomplete shifts will result in you not being invited back to camp with us in the future.
- **Strike:** **Strike is required.** You'll have a 3-hour shift over the final weekend (Saturday and/or Sunday) helping break camp down. **[NEEDS UPDATE: current-year strike dates]** If you have a special circumstance that will prevent this, contact the camp leads as early as possible and we'll work with you to accommodate your schedule. Simply skipping strike disqualifies you from camping with NODE, and a lifetime supply of love, laughter, and community. Please talk to us first. Leaving trash, unwanted items, and the like behind is not acceptable and will be considered when you ask to return.
- **Don't leave stuff behind:** Items may not be left at NODE without prior approval.

### How we think about dues

We think about camp dues a lot. Please have a read.

Going to Burning Man is just like going on any other holiday or vacation: partying in Ibiza, skiing in Vail, sightseeing in Europe. Some people can afford to hitchhike and couch surf or stay in a youth hostel, others fly in private jets and swing five-star hotels. Both of these and everything in between is perfect!

Our mission is to maintain a **diverse family** who have different skills, different resources, and life experiences. We're interested in WHO you are, not WHAT you are. In keeping with that spirit we operate a **self-selecting sliding-scale** dues process: we take the budget, divide it among our roughly 50 members, and distribute it across a sliding scale (with one or two variables).

Why run this experiment? It's one thing to say we support diversity. We believe it's important to create, implement, and test a system that supports it and can be scaled. Everyone should have the option to remain anonymous to the greater group about their contribution, and no one should feel indebted to a single individual for paying for them. If you've been invited to camp at NODE, it's because a significant number of people want you here, and we believe that you, merely by being YOU, contribute to the mission, vision, and greater collective.

**Everyone pays something. Everyone participates.** Paying higher dues does not exclude you from participation (build, shifts, strike); paying lower dues does not come with the co-relative requirement of increased participation. We have allocated some partial scholarships for those who can't cover their dues, prioritizing returning members and then new members on a first-come, first-served basis. We're in it together, trusting each other to do what we can. A little bit of science + a little bit of art = a lot a bit of magic.

### What it costs

Putting on our camp and making this holiday happen is an expensive endeavor. The hot breakfasts and dinners, electricity, showers, toilets, storage, transportation, and admin all add up.

- **Total camp budget:** **[NEEDS UPDATE: current camp budget]** (2025: ~$91,000)
- **Break-even per person:** **[NEEDS UPDATE: current per-person cost]** (2025: $1,725/person)

The entire budget goes to getting us onto and off playa. There is no profit, and for the past few years NODE has run in the negatives, kept alive by some extremely generous support. If you're able to make a larger contribution, please do consider doing so.

**Decommodification:** Everyone in our crew contributes in various ways. None of the camp leads are paid to run the camp; we pay dues like everyone else. None of our camp members are paid for their participation at Burning Man. We do pay expenses for our nourishment (kitchen) team, because they (like the folks who supply water, power, and shade) dedicate time on playa working in the same area as their day jobs. But we do NOT pay anyone in the kitchen, and they do NOT work for you.

## First-Timer Protips

*A few tips to make your first burn smoother.*

None of these are mandatory, but every one comes from NODEs who learned the hard way. Skim them before you pack.

### Seal a bag of clean clothes

When you're dirty and covered in dust, there is truly no respite like putting on a totally fresh, clean item of clothing. Some people only seal their final going-home outfit, so they're clean as they leave BRC. Others seal a few outfits throughout the week, which may just get you through some tough times at the burn. It takes a bit more planning and organization, but it pays dividends over the course of the trip. Just be sure to reuse the bags to minimize plastic waste.

### Take care of your feet (and vinegar baths)

Your feet are your #1 tool on the playa, and you should take extremely diligent care of them. Make sure your toenails are clipped before the trip, and pack functional closed shoes with lots of comfortable socks.

"Playa foot" is a malady unique to the Black Rock Desert caused by the alkali dust. It is, in essence, a chemical burn caused by exposing bare skin to the desert floor by not wearing socks and closed shoes. At NODE we believe the concern is a *little* overblown, and some breathing time is almost necessary to keep feet dry and healthy. We even have some campers who prefer sandals or spend time barefoot. HOWEVER, if you do let your feet touch the dust, it's critical you spend some extra time cleaning them off and treating them with a vinegar foot bath. The vinegar neutralizes the alkali, and you can add ice to help with soreness. More on playa foot: [Burning Man's playa foot guide](https://burningman.org/event/preparation/health-safety/playa-foot/).

### Glue your cuticles

Your hand and finger maintenance is also paramount at the burn. You'll be doing a lot of manual labor in a hot, dry environment, with fine dust getting in every crack and crevasse. Any cut or scratch you get will NOT heal until you return home where moisture exists, and hangnails will just tear and get worse. First thing when you arrive at NODE, take some super glue and apply a *small* amount to your cuticles to seal the gap between your skin and nails. It's a superhuman protective coating that doesn't come off with hand-washing or dry skin.

### Put a printed ID on your cup

This is just how it's done in BRC. To reduce plastic waste, every single individual at the burn carries a reusable cup at *all* times, and in a lot of cases that cup will be filled with alcohol, by a stranger! Enjoying a drink at another camp's bar is one of the simple joys of Burning Man, but you'll *always* need to present ID to show you're 21+. Burning Man even insists you should have your actual driver's license present, and a photocopy isn't admissible. But in 99% of cases, a printed copy of your license on your cup will do the job. Make sure you fully cover it with duct tape or it'll surely fade or tear.

### Rehearse your tent setup at home

There's nothing worse than getting to BM on Day 1, all excited to get settled, then fumbling with ropes and stakes for an hour in the heat. Especially if it's a new tent or you're unfamiliar with the setup, do a dry run at home. Toss it up in your backyard, brainstorm a layout for all your crap, and roll up to NODE knowing you'll be at home in no time.

### Put camp info on your phone's lock screen

A simple but genius failsafe in case you lose your phone on the playa. Set your lock screen to a photo of text showing your camp name, location, and maybe some other contact info. Most people in BRC are good samaritans, and if they find your lost phone with return instructions right there, they'll make it happen.

### Make your bike recognizable

Your bike is your trusty steed and the primary way around the event, but *everyone* will have one. Any time you arrive at an art piece in deep playa and disembark, your bike will be in a tangled mess of a thousand others, hard to relocate in the pitch black of night. Prepare a distinct decoration or two to spot yours easily, and ideally a bright light or a tail pole like a flag or LED stick.

**Lock buddy:** Lock your bike to a friend's rather than to a rack. That way two people remember the spot, and you've got double the custom decorations to home in on.

## Bikes

*How to get a bike to the playa and home again.*

Your bike is your primary transportation across Black Rock City. A few things up front:

**NODE does not provide bikes. We do not have extra bikes.** You'll need to buy or rent one, individually or through a service, bring it to Burning Man, **AND BRING IT HOME** (or donate it properly; see below).

> **[NEEDS UPDATE: verify current prices].** The dollar figures below are from a prior year. Confirm before relying on them.

### Analog (pedal) bikes

- **Kiwanis:** [kiwanisbikes.org](https://www.kiwanisbikes.org/burning-man.html#on-site_sales)
  - ~$120-150, walk-in or reservation (reservations may sell out)
  - Tons of bikes, cruisers, etc.
  - Pickup next to the airport
  - Donate after to a fire department, airport, or Kiwanis for free
- **Playa Bike Repair:** [playabikerepair.com](https://playabikerepair.com/products/burning-man-bike-rental)
  - ~$175, Citi-Bike-style bikes
  - Pickup/return in Reno. Must be returned or face a $300+ fee
  - If 10+ people get together, you get a free trailer to tow them in
- **Walmart**
  - ~$80+
  - Donate after to any Walmart, Kiwanis, fire department, airport, etc.

### Electric bikes

NODE does not have extra e-bikes, rentals, etc. You're on your own to sort one out. A couple of options:

- **Ship it:** You can ship your bike (and batteries) to the burn and back. One service: [nyccontainer.org](https://nyccontainer.org/) (originally set up for NY-based burners).
- **Rent:** [Dustrentals.com](https://dustrentals.com)

**Rules:** If you're purchasing an e-bike, make sure it's **under 750W** to comply with Burning Man rules. <!-- editor: original linked a BM e-bike rules bookmark; re-add the current official link -->

### Getting your bike there

- **Driving:** Strap it to your car.
- **RV:** Rent a bike rack or shove them inside.
- **Burner Express:** ~$25 on the bus, ~$150 on flights. [Burner Express info](https://burnerexpress.burningman.org/burner-express-bus-information/).

### Don't abandon your bike

There's a **$200 fee if you leave your bike behind** at camp. Plan your bike's exit as carefully as its arrival, or donate it through one of the options above.
